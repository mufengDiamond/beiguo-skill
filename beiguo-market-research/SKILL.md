# Beiguo Market Research

Use this skill to investigate a market as a living system, not as a survey form. Work from one concrete market until its people, goods, money, routes, rules, history, and conflicts become explainable.

## Core Principle

Do not begin with opinions. Begin with a decision problem, choose a typical field site, find people who know the inside story, verify details across roles, calculate the business, and explain the mechanism.

The investigation must be anchored in the user's own business context. Before giving conclusions, identify the user's product or service, target users, current customers, channel, pricing, delivery model, industry/category, geography, stage, constraints, and decision horizon. A market report is not finished until it explains what the evidence means for this specific user, not just for "the market" in general.

Use the phrase "Beiguo-style" to mean:

- Deep sample over shallow coverage.
- Inside informants over convenient respondents.
- Concrete categories, prices, volumes, routes, and rules over abstract market size claims.
- Historical change and power structure over static snapshots.
- User-context fit over generic industry advice.
- Product decisions over descriptive research.
- Real substitutes, buying chains, willingness to pay, and channel access over "user needs" as a slogan.
- Explicit uncertainty and missing evidence.

## Workflow

1. Define the decision problem.
   - State what decision this research must support.
   - Capture the user's business context: product/service, user group, industry/category, price point, channel, geography, stage, resources, constraints, and what they are trying to decide.
   - Name the user's current blind spots.
   - Convert vague goals like "understand the market" into 3-7 answerable research questions.

2. Choose a typical field site.
   - Select one city, district, store cluster, platform niche, channel, user segment, or category as the primary sample.
   - Explain why it is representative, extreme, strategic, or diagnostic.
   - Avoid broad coverage until one site is understood in depth.

3. Build the informant map.
   - Include insiders from different positions: merchants, users, channel operators, competitors, frontline staff, brokers/intermediaries, regulators/platform operators, old practitioners, and people hurt by the system.
   - For each informant type, state what they are likely to know and what bias they may have.
   - Prefer repeated interviews and group verification when feasible.

4. Draw the market map before drawing conclusions.
   - Map geography, traffic/logistics, upstream supply, downstream demand, middlemen, stores/platforms, seasonal nodes, and choke points.
   - Separate "local consumption", "through traffic", "wholesale/distribution", and "export/outbound" flows when relevant.

5. Decompose by category and transaction.
   - For each important product/service category, capture source, destination, buyer, seller, volume, price, gross margin, credit terms, seasonality, taxes/fees/platform cuts, labor, inventory, and constraints.
   - List small categories too when they reveal demand structure or cultural habits.

6. Calculate the business.
   - Use daily/weekly/monthly volume, price, frequency, and seasonality to estimate annual value.
   - Show assumptions and lower/upper bounds.
   - Cross-check numbers through tax, rent, inventory, staff count, receipt/order volume, or competitor benchmarks.

7. Analyze people and power.
   - Segment people by economic role, not only demographics: producers, sellers, intermediaries, consumers, laborers, landlords, platforms, lenders, regulators, and idle/edge groups.
   - Ask who earns, who pays, who depends on whom, who has bargaining power, and who is excluded.

8. Recover history and trend.
   - Compare the market at several moments: past peak, recent change, current state, likely next shift.
   - Identify what changed: technology, logistics, regulation, capital, substitute products, income, culture, credit, or competition.

9. Extract the mechanism.
   - Explain why the market operates this way.
   - Distinguish symptoms from causes.
   - Connect physical routes, transaction economics, institutions, social groups, and historical change.

10. Fit the evidence to the user's business.
   - Compare the market mechanism with the user's product/service, target users, current customers, channel, pricing, delivery capacity, trust assets, and constraints.
   - State where the user has fit, misfit, leverage, weak evidence, and hidden risk.
   - Translate the report into decision implications for this user: enter, avoid, narrow the segment, change offer, adjust price, choose channel, run a pilot, interview a missing group, or delay judgment.

11. Convert market facts into product research.
   - Identify the user's job-to-be-done: what progress the user is trying to make, in what trigger situation, under what constraints.
   - Map the current substitute path: competitors, manual work, Excel, agencies, communities, relationships, offline middlemen, doing nothing, or internal workarounds.
   - Separate user, buyer, payer, influencer, approver, and person who bears failure risk.
   - Grade demand strength: urgent pain, high-frequency habit, costly compliance, status/identity need, convenience improvement, curiosity, or weak nice-to-have.
   - Estimate willingness to pay from current spend, avoided loss, time saved, revenue gained, risk reduced, or prestige/status value.

12. Score the product opportunity.
   - Use a decision matrix with pain intensity, frequency, budget, current spend, switching cost, trust barrier, channel access, delivery difficulty, competition/substitution, repeat/retention potential, referral potential, and fit with the user's capabilities.
   - Do not call something an opportunity unless there is a plausible path to reach users, convert buyers, deliver reliably, and earn enough margin.
   - Distinguish "market is large", "users complain", "buyers pay", and "this user can win"; these are four different claims.

13. Design the next validation experiment.
   - Propose the smallest credible MVP, concierge test, landing page, paid preorder, sales call, prototype demo, field visit, or manual service test.
   - Name the first 5-20 target users or informant types, the recruitment channel, the offer, the price/test ask, and the pass/fail threshold.
   - State what evidence would make the user proceed, pivot, narrow the segment, change pricing, or stop.

14. Produce actionable outputs.
   - Deliver a research plan, interview guide, fieldwork checklist, synthesis report, opportunity map, risk map, or strategic recommendation depending on the user's request.
   - Mark confidence levels and missing evidence.

## Output Shapes

For a research plan, include:

- Decision problem
- User business context to collect or confirm
- Scope and typical sample
- Research questions
- Informant map
- Fieldwork sequence
- Data to collect
- Product hypotheses to test
- Substitute paths and buying-chain questions
- MVP or validation experiment
- Verification method
- Expected outputs

For a synthesis report, include:

- Executive judgment
- User-context fit: product/service, target user, channel, pricing, constraints
- Market map
- Category-by-category economics
- User and stakeholder structure
- Channel and flow analysis
- Historical change
- Core mechanism
- Product research: jobs-to-be-done, substitutes, buying chain, demand strength, willingness to pay
- Opportunity score: pain, budget, channel, delivery, competition, retention, capability fit
- User-specific conclusions: do/do not do, first segment, offer, price, channel, MVP, risks, and open questions

For an interview guide, use `references/interview-guide.md`.

## Quality Bar

- Use concrete units: people, stores, orders, kilograms, baskets, invoices, yuan, percentage cuts, trips, days, seasons.
- Preserve respondent language, local terms, channel slang, and transaction rituals.
- Cross-check any important claim from at least two roles or one role plus observable evidence.
- Avoid pretending precision. Label estimates as rough, conservative, or unverified.
- Do not give generic conclusions. Every recommendation must connect at least one market finding to at least one part of the user's product, service, user group, industry/category, channel, pricing, capability, or constraint.
- Do not say "there is demand" without specifying the trigger, substitute, buyer, payer, current spend, urgency, frequency, and evidence level.
- End strategic reports with a clear product decision: do, do not do, narrow, change offer, change price, change channel, validate first, or wait for more evidence.
- Always state what the investigation has not yet covered.

## Evidence Levels

Use evidence grades when making claims:

- A: Real payment, order, contract, retention, usage log, or observed transaction.
- B: Cross-verified by multiple roles or by one role plus observable evidence.
- C: Single-role interview claim, plausible but not yet verified.
- D: Public report, media source, platform data, or industry benchmark.
- E: Inference or hypothesis. Useful for planning, not for final certainty.
