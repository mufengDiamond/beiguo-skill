# Beiguo-Style Interview Guide

Use this reference to create concrete interview plans, fieldwork checklists, and report templates.

## User Context Intake

Before designing interviews or writing conclusions, capture the user's own situation:

- Product/service: what is actually sold or delivered, including format, price, margin, and fulfillment.
- User group: current users, target users, excluded users, buyer vs user, decision maker, and usage frequency.
- Industry/category: where the offer sits, what substitutes it competes with, and which category rules matter.
- Channel: acquisition path, sales channel, delivery channel, trust mechanism, and repeat-purchase path.
- Stage and constraints: current scale, resources, geography, team capacity, regulatory limits, cash/time constraints, and decision horizon.

Use these facts to shape research questions and final recommendations. Do not let the report end at general market description.

## Interview Roles

Interview across the market structure:

- Merchant/operator: prices, margin, sourcing, customer mix, seasonality, credit, inventory, hidden costs.
- Buyer/user: use case, purchase trigger, alternatives, budget, frequency, decision maker, pain.
- Channel/intermediary: flow, routing, commissions, bottlenecks, trust, dispute handling.
- Frontline worker: real process, daily volume, common failures, customer objections.
- Supplier/upstream: production constraints, wholesale price, payment terms, allocation logic.
- Competitor/adjacent operator: category boundaries, substitution, differentiation, survival logic.
- Regulator/platform/property owner: fees, rules, permits, enforcement, incentives.
- Old practitioner/local historian: past peak, major shocks, route changes, vanished categories.
- Weak/edge participant: excluded users, debtors, casual workers, failed merchants, lost customers.

## Question Bank

Start with facts before opinions:

- What exactly do you sell/buy/use?
- Where does it come from, and where does it go?
- Who pays whom, when, and by what terms?
- How much moves on a normal day, busy day, slow day, and peak season?
- What is the buying price, selling price, gross margin, rent, labor, fees, tax, platform cut, spoilage, return rate, or bad debt?
- Which products are the real volume drivers? Which products are prestige, habit, or add-on sales?
- What changed compared with 3 years ago, 10 years ago, or the previous generation?
- Who used to make money here and no longer does?
- Who controls access, traffic, credit, location, inventory, data, or certification?
- What do outsiders usually misunderstand about this business?
- What would break the business if it changed tomorrow?

## Product Research Question Bank

Use these questions when the user needs product research, monetization judgment, MVP design, or go-to-market decisions:

- What job is the user trying to get done, and what event triggers the search for a solution?
- What do they use today instead: competitor product, manual service, spreadsheet, community, relationship, middleman, internal staff, or doing nothing?
- What is bad, costly, slow, risky, embarrassing, or unreliable about the current substitute?
- Who discovers the solution, who uses it, who influences the choice, who approves it, who pays, and who bears the risk if it fails?
- What is the current spend in money, time, labor, opportunity cost, lost revenue, compliance risk, or reputation risk?
- How often does the problem happen, how urgent is it when it happens, and what happens if it is not solved?
- What price, contract length, payment method, or result-based fee would feel normal relative to existing spend?
- Where do these users gather, who already has their trust, and what channel can reach them at a cost below expected margin?
- What must be true for the user to switch, and what proof, credential, guarantee, integration, or relationship would reduce trust friction?
- What would a small paid test look like: preorder, deposit, concierge service, manual pilot, prototype demo, sales call, landing page, or field trial?

Ask for examples:

- Tell me about yesterday's transactions.
- Walk me through one order from source to final buyer.
- Show me a typical receipt, purchase list, shelf, order screen, delivery route, or customer conversation if possible.
- Name one good season and one bad season. What happened?
- Name one failed operator. Why did they fail?

## Field Observation Checklist

Record observable evidence:

- Location and traffic: entrances, routes, parking, loading, queue, footfall, delivery movement.
- Goods: SKU count, shelf position, freshness, packaging, origin labels, substitutes.
- Price: posted price, negotiated price, bundle price, wholesale price, credit price.
- People: buyer types, seller roles, labor division, repeat customers, brokers.
- Rhythm: opening hours, market days, rush periods, seasonal cycles.
- Friction: waiting, bargaining, inspection, returns, disputes, compliance checks.
- Artifacts: menus, invoices, contracts, order screenshots, membership rules, tax/fee notices.

## Calculation Template

Use rough math, but show assumptions:

```text
Daily volume x average price x active days = annual transaction value
Gross revenue - cost of goods - rent - labor - fees - loss/bad debt = estimated profit
Peak season volume / normal season volume = seasonality multiplier
Observed count x market nodes = market estimate
Tax/fee collected / fee rate = implied transaction value
```

Cross-check with:

- Supplier shipment volume
- Store inventory turnover
- Number of operators
- Staff workload
- Platform order count
- Tax/fee/rent data
- Customer purchase frequency
- Competitor benchmark

## Product Opportunity Scorecard

Score each opportunity as high, medium, low, or unknown:

- Pain intensity: how bad is the problem when it appears?
- Frequency: how often does it appear?
- Budget/current spend: what money or cost already exists?
- Buyer clarity: is the payer identifiable and reachable?
- Substitute weakness: why are current solutions insufficient?
- Switching cost: what makes changing hard?
- Trust barrier: what proof is required before purchase?
- Channel access: where can the user be reached repeatedly?
- Delivery difficulty: can the user's team fulfill reliably?
- Competition/substitution: who or what blocks adoption?
- Retention/referral: will users repeat, renew, or recommend?
- Capability fit: does this match the user's assets, team, geography, and constraints?

## Evidence Grades

Mark important claims with a grade:

- A: Real payment, order, contract, retention, usage log, or observed transaction.
- B: Cross-verified by multiple roles or by one role plus observable evidence.
- C: Single-role interview claim, plausible but not yet verified.
- D: Public report, media source, platform data, or industry benchmark.
- E: Inference or hypothesis.

## Report Skeleton

Use this outline for a concise final report:

1. Decision problem and answer
2. User context: product/service, users, category, channel, pricing, stage, constraints
3. Why this sample is diagnostic
4. Market map: routes, nodes, actors
5. Category economics: source, volume, price, margin, seasonality
6. User and stakeholder structure
7. Product research: job-to-be-done, substitutes, buying chain, demand strength, willingness to pay
8. Rules, fees, credit, and power
9. Historical change and trend
10. Mechanism: why the market works this way
11. Fit to the user's business: leverage, misfit, risk, and missing evidence
12. Opportunity scorecard
13. MVP or next validation experiment
14. User-specific strategic implications
15. Unknowns and next fieldwork

## Common Failure Modes

Avoid:

- Asking only managers and missing frontline reality.
- Reporting adjectives without quantities.
- Treating market size as a spreadsheet number without explaining flow.
- Confusing a passing-through market with local demand.
- Ignoring credit, fees, taxes, rent, spoilage, or labor.
- Flattening "users" into demographics instead of economic roles.
- Writing generic market conclusions that do not name the user's product, service, users, channel, pricing, or constraints.
- Saying "users need this" without identifying the trigger, current substitute, payer, buying chain, and evidence grade.
- Recommending a product without a channel, price logic, delivery path, and next validation experiment.
- Hiding uncertainty.
